1. What is Diabetes?
Diabetes is a chronic condition where the body cannot properly regulate blood glucose levels. It occurs when the pancreas does not produce enough insulin or when the body becomes resistant to insulin.

2. Types of Diabetes
Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition where the body destroys insulin‑producing cells.
Type 2 diabetes is the most common form and is caused by insulin resistance and reduced insulin production.
Gestational diabetes occurs during pregnancy and increases future risk of Type 2 diabetes.

3. What is Blood Glucose?
Blood glucose is the amount of sugar circulating in the bloodstream. It is the body’s main source of energy. High glucose levels indicate impaired insulin function.

4. Normal Blood Glucose Ranges
Fasting glucose between 70–99 mg/dL is normal.
Prediabetes is 100–125 mg/dL.
Diabetes is diagnosed at 126 mg/dL or higher.

5. What Does High Glucose Mean?
High glucose (hyperglycemia) means the body is not using insulin effectively. Persistent high glucose damages blood vessels, nerves, kidneys, and eyes.

6. Symptoms of Diabetes
Common symptoms include frequent urination, excessive thirst, fatigue, blurred vision, slow wound healing, and unexplained weight loss.

7. What is Insulin?
Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that helps move glucose from the blood into cells for energy.

8. What is Insulin Resistance?
Insulin resistance occurs when cells do not respond properly to insulin. The pancreas produces more insulin to compensate, but over time this leads to high blood sugar and Type 2 diabetes.

9. What is BMI?
Body Mass Index (BMI) is a measure of body fat based on height and weight.
BMI = weight (kg) / height² (m²).

10. How BMI Affects Diabetes Risk
Higher BMI increases insulin resistance. Obesity is one of the strongest predictors of Type 2 diabetes.

11. Blood Pressure and Diabetes
High blood pressure increases the risk of diabetes and cardiovascular complications.
Normal blood pressure is around 120/80 mmHg.

12. Lifestyle Changes to Reduce Diabetes Risk
Regular exercise, weight loss, balanced diet, reduced sugar intake, quitting smoking, limiting alcohol, and managing stress all reduce diabetes risk.

13. Diet and Diabetes Prevention
A healthy diet includes whole grains, vegetables, lean proteins, and high‑fiber foods. Reducing refined carbohydrates and sugary drinks lowers risk.

14. Physical Activity Recommendations
At least 150 minutes of moderate exercise per week improves insulin sensitivity and reduces diabetes risk.

15. Age and Diabetes Risk
Risk increases with age due to reduced insulin sensitivity and metabolic changes.

16. Family History and Genetics
A family history of diabetes increases risk. Genetics influence insulin production and glucose metabolism.

17. Complications of Diabetes
Long‑term complications include heart disease, kidney failure, nerve damage, vision loss, and poor circulation.

18. How Diabetes is Diagnosed
Diagnosis is based on fasting glucose, A1C test, or oral glucose tolerance test.

19. A1C Ranges
Normal A1C is below 5.7%.
Prediabetes is 5.7–6.4%.
Diabetes is 6.5% or higher.

20. What is Prediabetes?
Prediabetes is a condition where blood sugar is elevated but not high enough for a diabetes diagnosis. It is reversible with lifestyle changes.

21. Diabetes Risk Factors
Major risk factors include high BMI, age, family history, high blood pressure, high cholesterol, sedentary lifestyle, and poor diet.

22. Early Warning Signs
Early signs include fatigue, increased thirst, frequent urination, and elevated fasting glucose.

23. Diabetes Prevention
Prevention focuses on weight management, healthy eating, physical activity, and regular screening.

24. Machine Learning Features in Diabetes Datasets
Common features include glucose, BMI, age, blood pressure, insulin, pregnancies, skin thickness, and diabetes pedigree function.

25. Outcome Variable in Diabetes Prediction
The outcome variable is binary:
0 = No diabetes
1 = Diabetes present.

26. Most Predictive Features
Glucose is typically the strongest predictor.
BMI, age, and insulin levels also contribute significantly.

27. Skin Thickness and Diabetes
Skin thickness is used as a proxy for body fat and can correlate with insulin resistance.

28. Diabetes Pedigree Function
This feature estimates genetic risk based on family history.

29. Simple Explanation of Diabetes
Diabetes happens when your body cannot use sugar properly. Sugar stays in your blood instead of entering your cells.

30. Simple Explanation of Insulin Resistance
Your body makes insulin, but your cells stop responding to it. Sugar builds up in your blood.
